# Prologue
I would just like to preface this post by acknowledging that the Model United Nations circuit has been the most formative part of my life for the past 4 years. I have met many awesome people, learnt many things, not just in the realm of international relations, but also on working with others, as well about myself. It is here that I truly discovered my passion for teaching, for educating others. As I march towards the realm of retirement, I can't help but feel a twinge of grief — not just the grief that I might be leaving the circuit for the rest of my human life, but also a grief regarding the current state that the circuit is left in.
# Ew, Humanities.
if you had asked a Secondary 1 me what I thought of the humanities, I would have said that it is a fruitless pursuit and a waste of human effort. Looking back, I realised just how wrong I was.

I stumbled upon the concept of Model United Nations by accident: one day my amazing seniors decided to drag me to sit in for a humanities club session, where Model United Nations was brought to my attention. Young me was just merely fascinated by the idea of roleplaying as diplomats and pretending to make agreements on the international stage. Little did I know that this would become the (frankly inhumane) rabbit hole that I will dive into for the next 4 years of my life.

Participating in Model United Nations conferences has really opened my eyes to a world of humanities that I had never seen before. Here I saw the ideas of Literature, History, Geography, Economics, Politics, International Relations, all come together to answer a simple question: How should states position themselves in the international world? (Of which I still have yet to find the answer to, but that is beside the point.) It is here that I found what the humanities mean to me — a way to rationalise and perceive the qualitative aspects of human experiences. My Model United Nations experiences have left me a very interdisciplinary experience and perspective of the humanities, and that is something that I still, and will for the foreseeable future, hold very dear to my heart.

And that is why, for a period of time, I seriously considered devoting myself to studying the humanities. Because the humanities is able to help us capture and understand the human lived experience unlike anything the sciences has to offer. And that really resonated with me.
# Drift.
However, as the years go on and individuals in the circuit come and go, I noticed that the goals and objectives the Model United Nations circuit was pushing for has slightly drifted. No longer were the conversations I had in committee regarding projecting state interest and representing your assigned country well, but rather it has become more of who has the most "funny" props, who has the funniest speeches and Working Papers (for the benefit of those not familiar with Model United Nations proceedings, Working Papers are visual aids that one can screen during speeches), who has the funniest acronyms, and what are the funniest things we can make the committee/council do. Whist I have no qualms of having some fun in Model United Nations conferences (after all, the fun of roleplaying diplomats is part of the reason many people, including me, have stayed on to delegate at so many conferences), I still ultimately believe that the end goal, of any conference, is to have their delegates take away **at least something** after spending 3-4 days there.

Model United Nations has always been about simulating international proceedings to gain a deeper understanding of our world today. This is what truly has kept me coming back, not just as a delegate, but as a chairperson and an academics secretariat member. The opportunity to learn something new, to teach something new about our world to other delegates, has always struck me as the most meaningful part of Model United Nations, and is the core reason why I have stayed for 4 years. Unfortunately, as the focus of newcomers to the scene shift towards having the most fun and niche councils, such as fictional committees with little to no purpose, I fear that many won't be able to have as much as a meaningful takeaway from the Model United Nations endeavours as I did.

Even worse, Model United Nations is increasingly being framed as a place to farm for portfolio. Whilst yes, the learning takeaways from participating in committees/councils do aid you in your journey in the Humanities, they do not present themselves just because you participated in them. You gain as much from your Model United Nations experience as you put into it, and it really pains me as a chairperson when delegates do not interact with the materials and environment you put in hundreds of hours to craft, just for delegates to horse around for another line on their Linkedin.
# So What Now?
As people get old, they retire. Unfortunately my days in the circuit are numbered, given the looming threat of more demanding things such as national examinations and tertiary education. As the circuit progresses, and slides down the slippery slope of Model United Nations as a means to only fun and games, I feel that it is important for newcomers to this circuit to inculcate themselves with the correct mindset to approach this "hobby" in a meaningful manner.

I sincerely hope that Model United Nations can remain as a gateway to the humanities, such that even "haters" like me 4 years ago, can see it in a different light, and come to truly appreciate the purpose of the humanities. I hope, that 10, 20 years down the road, when younger students enter the circuit, they will be able to have as meaningful and as insightful of an experience as I had. But alas, this is all just a hope.

Thank you, Model United Nations, for help facilitating my self-growth over the past 4 years. Thank you for showing me the beauty and finesse of the humanities. And for what may possibly be the last time, see you around in the circuit.
